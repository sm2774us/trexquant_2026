### 🔬 D1 · How do you prevent overfitting in a systematic research process?

**Optimal Answer:**

Overfitting prevention is not a step at the end of my research process — it is the architectural constraint that governs every decision from hypothesis formation onward. Four layers:

1. **CPCV (Combinatorial Purged Cross-Validation):** I use CPCV as the primary validation protocol rather than simple train/test splits. It generates an unbiased distribution of backtested Sharpe ratios across combinatorially generated path subsets, with purge and embargo windows to eliminate leakage from serial autocorrelation.

2. **Multiple Testing Correction:** When testing a library of signals or feature variants, I apply Benjamini-Hochberg correction to control the False Discovery Rate. For individual strategy-level decisions, I use the Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR) to account for selection bias across the hypothesis search space.

3. **Walk-Forward + Out-of-Sample Gating:** No signal is approved for production consideration until it demonstrates positive out-of-sample performance on an untouched holdout — typically the most recent 20–25% of available history, never touched during development.

4. **Regime Sparsity Audit:** I decompose signal performance by macro regime (expansion, contraction, high/low volatility, risk-on/risk-off via HMM segmentation) to confirm alpha is not regime-concentrated. A signal that only works in one regime is not alpha — it is a regime bet with a backtest artifact attached.

### 🤖 D2 · How do you approach combining many weak signals into a portfolio?

**Optimal Answer:**

Signal combination is where research rigor translates into realized Sharpe. My approach is layered:

1. **Orthogonality Testing First:** I compute pairwise IC (Information Coefficient) correlations across the signal library. Signals with high IC correlation are redundant — combining them adds noise, not diversification. I prune or orthogonalize via residualization before weighting.

2. **Ensemble Weighting Methods:** For combining orthogonal signals, I use three approaches depending on signal count and data richness: (a) equal-weight as a robust baseline, (b) IC-weighted (by rolling out-of-sample IC stability), and (c) Ridge regression with cross-validated regularization to prevent weight concentration.

3. **Carry / Trend / Value / Volatility Decomposition:** At the macro level, I decompose the signal library into orthogonal economic factor families — Carry, Trend, Value, and Volatility Risk Premium. This ensures that even if individual signals within a family degrade, the portfolio maintains structural diversification across economic regimes.

4. **Dynamic Regime Adjustment:** I use HMM-derived regime probabilities to dynamically tilt signal weights. In high-volatility regimes, trend signals historically dominate; in low-volatility, carry and mean-reversion tend to outperform. This regime-conditional weighting is always validated out-of-sample.

### 📊 D3 · Describe a signal you built from alternative or non-traditional data.

**Optimal Answer:**

At Highbridge, I built a short-horizon price reversion signal from limit order book (LOB) microstructure data. The core feature was **Order Flow Imbalance (OFI)** — the net signed volume at the best bid and ask over rolling micro-windows (100ms to 5s). The signal captured adverse selection: when OFI was persistently directional, it identified informed flow, which historically predicted short-term price continuation; when OFI was noisy and mean-reverting, it identified liquidity-driven dislocations, which predicted reversion.

Key construction steps:
- Extracted OFI features at multiple granularities (tick, 500ms, 1s, 5s) and used PCA to collapse them into orthogonal microstructure factors.
- Applied an LSTM classifier (PyTorch) to sequence the OFI factor states and predict the next-period mid-price direction.
- Validated the resulting signal with CPCV, confirming positive IC out-of-sample after transaction cost modeling (TCM) against Implementation Shortfall benchmarks.

The signal survived live paper-trading with an annualized IC of ~0.07 — modest but consistent and additive to the existing factor library after orthogonalization.

### 🌐 D4 · How do you think about signal decay and when to retire a strategy?

**Optimal Answer:**

Signal decay is the natural equilibrium response to alpha being arbitraged away. My monitoring framework has three tiers:

| Tier | Metric | Threshold for Review | Threshold for Retirement |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Statistical** | Rolling 6-month out-of-sample IC vs. historical IC distribution | IC drops below 50th percentile | IC p-value > 0.10 for 3 consecutive quarters |
| **Economic** | Sharpe ratio under live execution vs. backtest | Live Sharpe < 60% of backtested Sharpe | Live Sharpe < 0 for 2 consecutive quarters |
| **Regime** | Signal performance decomposed by HMM regime state | Performance confined to 1 regime | No positive IC in any of 3+ identified regimes |

When a signal crosses review thresholds, I first investigate whether the decay is structural (regime shift, crowding, regulatory change) or transient (data quality issues, liquidity regime). Structural decay triggers retirement and replacement research. Transient decay triggers parameter recalibration under strict out-of-sample constraints — never curve-fitting to the decay period.
